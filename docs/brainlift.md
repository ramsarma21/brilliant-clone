# BrainLift: Learning Physics by Thinking With Systems

## Owners

- Ram Sarma

## Purpose

The purpose of this BrainLift is to develop a strong point of view on how students should learn introductory physics: not by memorizing formulas, but by building a felt understanding of how physical systems behave.

This BrainLift is not about feature requirements or build details. It is about the deeper educational idea behind the project:

What if physics learning was designed around helping students think with systems before asking them to calculate answers?

The project explores how an interactive learning experience can help students form intuition, test assumptions, notice cause and effect, and develop confidence in reasoning about the physical world.

## DOK 4: Spiky Points of View

### Spiky POV 1: Physics is not hard because students cannot do the math. Physics is hard because students do not trust their own mental models.

Many students approach physics as if every problem is a code to decode: find the right equation, insert the numbers, hope the answer looks reasonable. This is not really physics thinking. The deeper problem is that students do not feel ownership over a mental model of the situation. They do not confidently know what should happen when speed changes, when mass changes, when friction appears, or when a circuit opens.

The project should treat physics learning as model-building, not formula selection. A student should first learn to ask: “What do I expect this system to do?” Only after that expectation exists does the formula become meaningful. The formula should refine intuition, not replace it.

### Spiky POV 2: A good physics lesson should make the student wrong in a useful way.

The most valuable learning moment is not when the student gets an answer correct. It is when their prediction fails and they can see exactly why. Physics misconceptions are often invisible because students can hide behind memorized procedures. An interactive lesson should surface those misconceptions quickly and safely.

If a student thinks a heavier object always moves faster down a ramp, the lesson should let them test that belief. If they think a steeper projectile always travels farther, the lesson should make the tradeoff visible. The goal is not to avoid mistakes. The goal is to design mistakes that reveal the structure of the concept.

### Spiky POV 3: Interactivity without reflection is just motion on a screen.

A simulation alone does not teach. Students can drag sliders, watch animations, and still leave with the same misconception they started with. Interactivity becomes learning only when the student is asked to predict, compare, explain, and revise.

The project should not worship “sandbox” learning as automatically better. A sandbox is valuable only when it is shaped around a question. The learner needs moments of freedom, but also moments of commitment: “What do you think will happen?” “Why did that happen?” “What changed your mind?” Without that structure, the student is entertained but not transformed.

### Spiky POV 4: The best physics learning experiences should feel less like being taught and more like gaining eyesight.

Introductory physics often fails because it describes invisible relationships with symbols before the student has any visual or physical grip on them. A strong interactive lesson should make relationships visible: slope becomes motion, force becomes acceleration, height becomes energy, resistance changes brightness.

The goal is not just to explain physics. The goal is to change what the student can notice. After a good lesson, the world should look slightly different. A thrown ball, a sliding box, a lamp circuit, or a hill should feel more readable.

### Spiky POV 5: The emotional design of physics matters as much as the conceptual design.

Students often carry an identity-level fear around physics: “I’m not a physics person.” That belief changes how they approach every problem. If the experience feels like a test, students protect themselves by guessing, rushing, or disengaging. If it feels like exploration, they are more willing to think.

The project should create a tone where being wrong feels like part of the experiment, not proof of inadequacy. The interface, pacing, feedback, and challenges should communicate: “Your intuition is not bad; it is unfinished.”

### Spiky POV 6: A physics app should not try to be a better textbook. It should be a better lab for the mind.

Textbooks are good at storing explanations. They are weak at creating experiences. A modern physics learning product should not simply move textbook paragraphs onto a screen with nicer visuals. It should create mental labs where students can run small experiments on ideas.

The app should help students develop habits of inquiry: isolate variables, predict outcomes, compare cases, notice patterns, and revise explanations. Those habits matter more than any single answer.

## DOK 3: Insights

### Insight 1: Students often memorize equations because they have no other object to hold onto.

Formula memorization is not just laziness. It is a coping mechanism. When a student cannot picture the system, the equation becomes the only stable thing in the room. A good learning experience must give them something better to hold: a visible model of cause and effect.

### Insight 2: Prediction is the doorway into real understanding.

If a student changes a variable without first predicting what will happen, they may not notice the meaning of the outcome. Prediction forces the student to reveal their current mental model. That makes feedback meaningful.

### Insight 3: The most important physics misconceptions are usually sensible overextensions of everyday experience.

Students are not randomly wrong. They bring everyday intuitions that work in some contexts and fail in others. For example, in daily life, objects often stop unless something keeps pushing them, so it feels natural to think force is required for motion. Physics education should respect these intuitions while refining them.

### Insight 4: Visual learning is powerful only when it shows relationships, not just pictures.

A nice animation of a projectile is not enough. The student needs to see how angle affects time in air, how speed affects range, and how horizontal and vertical motion can be separated. The value is not the image; it is the visible relationship.

### Insight 5: Confidence comes from repeated successful reasoning, not from being told the answer.

Students become confident when they experience themselves making sense of a system. The app should create small wins where the learner predicts, tests, adjusts, and sees that their reasoning improved.

### Insight 6: The best feedback should sound like it understands the learner’s misconception.

Generic feedback treats wrong answers as failures. Specific feedback treats wrong answers as evidence of a particular mental model. A strong physics learning experience should respond to the misconception underneath the answer.

### Insight 7: Learning physics means learning to ask “what changed?” before asking “what equation?”

A student who can identify the relevant change in a system is already thinking like a physicist. Did the mass change? Did the force change? Did the height change? Did the path open? The project should train attention toward relationships between variables.

## DOK 2: Knowledge Tree

### Category 1: Why Physics Feels Difficult

#### Raw facts and observations

- Students often know formulas but cannot explain when or why to use them.
- Introductory physics problems require translating words into a model of a situation.
- Many mistakes come from misconceptions about motion, force, energy, graphs, and circuits.
- Students may think getting stuck means they are “bad at physics.”

#### Summary

Physics difficulty is not only mathematical. It is conceptual, visual, emotional, and interpretive. Students need help forming a model of the situation before they can use math productively.

### Category 2: What “Learning by Doing” Means

#### Raw facts and observations

- Doing does not simply mean clicking or dragging.
- Productive doing includes prediction, manipulation, observation, explanation, and revision.
- A learner must see the consequence of their action clearly.
- A challenge gives the interaction purpose.

#### Summary

Learning by doing means the student actively tests and revises a mental model. Interaction is valuable when it creates a thought process, not just activity.

### Category 3: Physics Intuition

#### Raw facts and observations

- Intuition is built through repeated exposure to cause and effect.
- Students need to compare cases: more mass versus less mass, more friction versus less friction, steeper angle versus flatter angle.
- Intuition becomes stronger when students can explain why the outcome changed.

#### Summary

Physics intuition is pattern recognition grounded in causal understanding. The app should help students build that pattern recognition through structured experimentation.

### Category 4: Feedback and Misconceptions

#### Raw facts and observations

- Wrong answers often reveal coherent but incomplete reasoning.
- Good feedback identifies the misconception and points the learner back to the system.
- Students need feedback quickly enough to connect it to their action.
- Feedback should preserve confidence while correcting thinking.

#### Summary

Feedback should not merely judge. It should diagnose. The strongest feedback helps the learner see the difference between what they expected and what the system actually did.

### Category 5: The Role of Beauty and Polish

#### Raw facts and observations

- A clean interface reduces cognitive load.
- A polished interaction makes the learner more willing to explore.
- Visual hierarchy tells the student what matters.
- Excessive animation can distract from the concept.

#### Summary

Polish is not superficial. In an educational product, clarity and beauty can help students focus attention, feel safe experimenting, and take the lesson seriously.

## Core Mental Model

The project should be built around this learning loop:

1. Notice the system.
2. Predict what will happen.
3. Change one thing.
4. Observe the result.
5. Explain the difference.
6. Try again with a sharper model.

This loop is the heart of the project. Every lesson, interaction, and piece of feedback should support it.

## Principles for the Actual Project

1. Start with intuition before formal language.
2. Make invisible relationships visible.
3. Ask students to predict before showing the result.
4. Treat wrong answers as useful evidence.
5. Keep explanations short and tied to the learner’s action.
6. Design every interaction around a meaningful question.
7. Help students compare cases, not just inspect one case.
8. Make the student feel like they are discovering structure, not being lectured.
9. Use math as a tool for sharpening understanding, not as the first point of contact.
10. Build confidence by helping students experience their own reasoning improving.
